# The behavioural basis

Each trait treats a receipt signal as a window onto a known pattern of behaviour.
This section gives the research each one draws on. The point is to ground *why* a
signal is meaningful — not to claim the signal measures the concept exactly, and not
to publish the threshold that scores it.

## 2.1 Impulse — timing

The impulse trait reads the share of spend that lands on weekends or in the
evening/night. **Daniel Kahneman's** account of *System 1* — fast, automatic,
effortful-to-override thinking — describes the conditions under which unplanned
purchases surface: fatigue, low deliberation, off-hours. Work on emotional and
sleep-deprived spending (Marter, 2023; and contemporary consumer-finance research)
supports treating timing as a proxy for less-deliberated buying.

## 2.2 Hunter — discounts

The hunter trait reads the ratio of discounted items in the basket. **Richard
Thaler's** concept of *transaction utility* names the pleasure of a good deal as
distinct from the value of the item itself — a person can derive satisfaction from
the discount as such. **Dan Ariely's** work on *anchoring* explains how a reference
price reshapes what feels cheap. Together they make discount-seeking a coherent
behavioural axis.

## 2.3 Explorer — novelty

The explorer trait reads the rate of first-seen merchants. **Gladstone, Matz &
Lemaire (2019, *Psychological Science*)** show that spending patterns track
personality traits, including openness to experience. A rising rate of new merchants
is a direct behavioural correlate of that openness.

## 2.4 Hedonist — category mix

The hedonist trait reads the share of spend in hedonic categories — cafés, treats,
entertainment. **Dunn & Norton's** *Happy Money* distinguishes experiential from
material spending and documents their different relationship to hedonic adaptation;
**Kahneman's** work on adaptation underlies it. **Veblen's** *conspicuous
consumption* adds the social dimension: some spending signals status rather than
need.

## 2.5 Loyal — concentration

The loyal trait reads how concentrated visits are in a few merchants. **Charles
Duhigg's** *cue–routine–reward* loop describes how repeated visits harden into
automatic habits. **Pierre Bourdieu's** concept of *habitus* — durable dispositions
shaped by one's milieu — explains why people return to the same places as a matter
of settled taste rather than fresh choice.

## 2.6 Planner — essentials and cadence

The planner trait reads the essentials share together with a steady basket cadence.
**Thaler's** *mental accounting* describes how people budget by earmarking money into
categories; **Klontz et al.'s** *money vigilance* names a careful, planning stance
toward spending; **Mullainathan & Shafir's** *Scarcity* shows that under financial
constraint, essentials come to dominate the basket. A steady, essentials-heavy
pattern is the behavioural signature these describe.
