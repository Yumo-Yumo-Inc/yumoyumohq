# The question

## 1.1 What hidden cost measures

For a single line on a receipt — a litre of milk, a shirt, a tank of fuel — the
hidden-cost estimate asks: **how much of this price sits above the cost of
producing the good?** It is the gap between what a person paid and a reference
price built from production cost and a reasonable margin.

The estimate is a **gap**, not an accusation. A wide gap is not by itself evidence
of overcharging; distribution, retail operation, taxes, and brand all legitimately
occupy the space between factory and shelf. What the layer does is make that space
visible and attribute it to sourced statistics, so a person can see the shape of
what they routinely pay.

## 1.2 Why a receipt is the right unit

The receipt fixes four things that a reference price needs: the **good**, the
**price paid**, the **time**, and often the **merchant and city**. Because the good
is matched to a canonical product and the time is known, the reference can be drawn
from the price statistics that applied in that period rather than a generic
average. The estimate is therefore specific to the purchase, not to a category in
the abstract.

## 1.3 What the estimate does not claim

The layer reports a modelled reference, not a measured factory cost. It does not
claim to know any single producer's books. It reads published price statistics and
sector cost composition, and from those builds a reference that a typical purchase
can be compared against. Two honest consequences follow:

- The estimate is **directional**, most useful in aggregate and over time, where
  idiosyncratic differences between products wash out.
- Where the underlying statistics are thin or a category lacks a sourced cost
  composition, the layer reports that limit rather than papering over it. The
  [limits section](05-limitations.md) is explicit about where this happens.
